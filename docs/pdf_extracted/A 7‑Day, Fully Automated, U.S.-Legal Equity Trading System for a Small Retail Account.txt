A 7‑Day, Fully Automated, U.S.-Legal Equity
Trading System for a Small Retail Account
Executive Summary
You asked for a “similar” system to the event-contract arb bot: something  fully automated,  U.S.-legal, 
buildable in 7 days, runnable on a home Mac mini / PC, starting with $100–$1,000, and capable of making
substantial money.
A hostile, reality-based conclusion first:
In U.S. equities, the closest thing to “hard arbitrage” is essentially unavailable to a solo retail
bot because the edges that resemble arbitrage (latency/exchange-feed advantages, queue priority,
cross-venue microstructure games) require colocation, direct feeds, broker/dealer infrastructure,
and/or significant capital. A home computer using retail broker APIs is structurally too slow and
too far from the matching engines to compete in “market making” and “latency” categories. 
With $100–$1,000, you cannot rely on leverage via margin for intraday strategies, because U.S.
margin day trading is constrained by the Pattern Day Trader (PDT) framework: $25,000 minimum
equity is required to day trade as a “pattern day trader” in a margin account. 
Many “substantial money” claims are just unpriced tail risk (selling volatility, holding levered ETFs
through gaps) or survivorship bias. True persistent alpha for a tiny, fully automated retail equity bot
is usually single-digit bps/day at best, and often disappears after realistic slippage and data
limitations.
That said, you can build something that is:
legally deployable on regulated brokers,
fully autopilot,
robust enough to run continuously,
with a modest but plausible edge, primarily by avoiding the arenas where retail is structurally
disadvantaged.
Best V1 choice (brutally practical)
Best V1 strategy for a solo retail builder in 7 days:
Low-frequency trend + volatility targeting on a broad equity ETF (e.g., SPY/QQQ) executed once per
day (or once per week).
Why this is the “least bad” choice for week one:
It avoids PDT-landmines (few trades, no intraday scalping). 
It avoids expensive tick data and direct-feed requirements; you can run it off daily bars.

Regulatory constraints that dominate feasibility
Pattern Day Trader (PDT) constraint: If you “day trade” in a margin account as a pattern day trader ,
you must maintain $25,000 minimum equity or day trading is restricted. This is a hard constraint
for $100–$1,000 automation. 
Minimum equity for margin: FINRA rule text includes an equity requirement of at least $2,000 for
margin, and identifies $25,000 as the PDT minimum. 
Settlement cycle matters in cash accounts: The U.S. moved most broker-dealer transactions from 
T+2 to T+1 with a May 28, 2024 compliance date; this reduces but does not eliminate cash-account
“funds available” constraints.

Shorting is operationally hard at small scale: Short sales are regulated under Regulation SHO
(marking, locate, close-out, price test circuit breaker). Retail bots that depend on shorting face
borrow availability, recalls, and rule compliance at the broker layer . 
Implication: In week one, your bot should behave like a cash/1x account, mostly long-only, low turnover ,
and explicitly throttle trades to avoid both PDT classification and freeriding/settlement violations.
Realistic Alpha Sources for Retail Equities
This section deliberately separates (A) what is “real in academia,” (B) what is “retail-accessible,” and (C) what
is “buildable in 7 days.”
Market making
What  it  is: provide  bids/asks,  earn  spread  and  (sometimes)  rebates,  manage  inventory  and  adverse
selection.
Reality for a home retail bot: - True market making in U.S. equities is fundamentally about  latency +
queue priority + adverse selection control and requires order-book depth data and infrastructure closer
than retail APIs. Direct feeds such as Nasdaq TotalView-ITCH provide order-level data in high-performance
formats; SIP feeds consolidate quotes/trades across venues and publish NBBO, but do not give you the
same microstructure edge. 
- Even at the broker API layer , order acknowledgements can be on the order of tens to 100+ milliseconds
(IBKR cites an approximation of 75–130ms for order acknowledgement latency in one of its materials), which
is far too slow for competitive market making. 
- Regulation SHO contains market making exceptions for “bona fide market making,” which is not a retail
account posture; you don’t get the exemptions or the economics. 
Verdict: Real market making is not a viable retail edge with home hardware and $100–$1,000. It will mostly
be “adverse selection donation.”
Statistical arbitrage / pairs trading
What is real: short-term reversal and related effects have strong empirical documentation (e.g., New York
Fed staff work decomposing return reversal). 
Retail feasibility:
- Market-neutral stat arb typically requires shorting, financing, stable borrow, and enough diversification to
smooth noise—hard with $100–$1,000 and Regulation SHO constraints. 
- Long-only “pairs” variants exist (rotate between two correlated assets), but those often become  timing
strategies rather than true arbitrage.
Verdict: Not week-one optimal unless you stay long-only and accept lower “arb-ness.”

Event-driven: earnings drift
What  is  real: Post-earnings  announcement  drift  is  a  long-studied  anomaly;  the  canonical  Bernard  &
Thomas  work  is  widely  cited,  and  modern  literature  continues  to  analyze  post-announcement  drift
dynamics. 
Retail  feasibility  (7  days): -  The  hard  part  is  clean  event  data (earnings  timestamps,  standardized
“surprise” measures), and avoiding survivorship bias and lookahead. Doing this rigorously in a week is
difficult without paid data.
Verdict: Strong academically, but operationally hard for a robust autopilot build in 7 days unless you use a
simplified proxy (price gap + volume shock) and accept noise.
Retail order-flow exploitation
Reality: You do not see internalized retail flow; you see prints/quotes. Many retail “order flow” stories
conflate publicly visible tape prints with broker-dealer internal routing logic.
Verdict: Not a realistic retail edge as a data advantage. If you trade “retail flow,” you are trading price/
volume patterns, which collapses into momentum/reversal categories.
Volatility / options “volatility risk premium”
What  is  real: There  is  extensive  evidence  that  implied  volatility  tends  to  exceed  subsequent  realized
volatility (variance/volatility risk premium), forming the economic basis for option-selling strategies. 
Retail feasibility: - Harvesting VRP typically means  selling options (short vol), which introduces tail risk
and usually requires margin/options approvals and sufficient equity (often at least the FINRA-style $2k level
for margin accounts). 
- With $100–$1,000, your strategy becomes either (a) buying options (negative carry on average), or (b) tiny
defined-risk spreads with heavy frictions.
Verdict: Real edge exists, but “substantial money” usually means “substantial tail risk.” Not recommended
for week one.
Microstructure latency plays
Reality: Direct-feed and colocation-driven strategies require specialized access. SIP feeds publish NBBO and
regulatory info, but are not a latency edge versus direct feeds. 
Retail broker APIs are not designed for HFT speed; your orders traverse broker risk checks, routing, and the
public market structure. 
Verdict: Not viable for home hardware using public broker APIs.

The “retail-accessible” bucket that remains: momentum / trend / reversal
Cross-sectional momentum is strongly documented (e.g., buy winners, sell losers) in classic finance
research. 
Time-series momentum/trend is also strongly documented across liquid instruments and horizons. 
Short-term reversal mechanisms are also studied and can sometimes be implemented long-only via
timing/filters. 
This is the only category that is simultaneously: retail-accessible, implementable with cheap data, and
buildable in 7 days with reasonable operational reliability.
Data Requirements and Real Costs
What data you actually need (for a 7‑day V1)
For a low-frequency ETF trend/volatility strategy, you need only:
Daily OHLCV for your chosen ETFs
Brokerage account/positions
Reliable order placement + order status + fills
This can be sourced from the broker/brokerage API directly, without buying tick/TAQ datasets.
Why tick/NBBO data is a trap for week one
The U.S. equity market’s “official” consolidated picture is produced by the SIP infrastructure and
disseminates NBBO and regulatory bands (e.g., LULD). 
Direct feeds like Nasdaq TotalView-ITCH provide order-level depth and message-level updates—
useful for high-speed strategies, but operationally and financially heavy for a home build. 
“TAQ” style datasets provide tick-by-tick trade and quote across the National Market System and are
used in professional research, but are not necessary for—and usually not affordable/operationally
justified for—a small retail V1. 
Market data costs (what’s realistic for a retail bot)
“True” SIP redistribution and non-display use can be expensive at the institutional level; a Cboe
DataShop page illustrates that SIP fee schedules involve separate access, usage, and non-display
categories with large monthly figures. 
Some brokers offer low-cost packaged real-time feeds; others provide IEX-only free data in paper
environments. For example, Alpaca notes paper-only accounts are entitled to IEX market data. 
Practical guidance for V1:
Use broker-provided quote/bars or a low-cost retail data plan that is clearly documented, and avoid building
a “professional market data stack” until you have a proven strategy.

One Best V1 Strategy
Strategy selection rationale (quant PM + hostile reviewer)
Given your constraints, the “single best” week-one equities bot should:
Trade liquid instruments to minimize spread and execution uncertainty.
Avoid requiring margin or shorting.
Trade infrequently to bypass PDT risk and to reduce the importance of intraday microstructure.
Be built on a phenomenon that is well documented and not purely narrative.
Chosen V1:
Time-series momentum (trend) + volatility targeting on a broad equity ETF.
Academic support for trend/time-series momentum as a return pattern (across liquid assets) is strong. 
Cross-sectional momentum in equities is also well documented. 
This does not guarantee profit. It is the best tradeoff between implementability and plausibility.
Expected edge mechanism
This strategy’s “edge” (if it exists going forward) is attributed to:
underreaction / delayed aggregation of information (one of the interpretations of momentum), 
behavioral and institutional flow persistence,
risk premia for crash exposure (trend tends to underperform during sharp reversals).
You are not “outsmarting Wall Street.” You are harvesting a broad, historically persistent return structure
with simple, low-cost execution.

Broker/regulatory constraints:
Do not place intraday round trips; this strategy trades at close only (no day trades), minimizing PDT

No-trade conditions (must be explicit)
No trading if market data is stale or missing (e.g., no valid close for day t).
No trading if broker API is degraded (timeouts, repeated rejects).
No trading during abnormal market halts for the instrument (you won’t reliably detect all halts
without deeper feeds; safest is “if orders rejected / no quotes, halt execution”).
Slippage and transaction cost assumptions (conservative for planning)
For a highly liquid ETF, model:
2 bps per round-trip (spread + slippage + minor fees).
This is an assumption, not a guaranteed fill quality.
Regulatory pass-through fees exist (e.g., SEC/FINRA fees on sells at some brokers). Alpaca’s fee schedule
explicitly lists items like FINRA Trading Activity Fees (TAF) and SEC fees on sells, even if commission is $0.
Expected trade frequency
Daily version: ~0–10 rebalances per month depending on volatility changes and signal flips (most
days changes are small if you apply the $25 minimum trade filter).
Weekly version: 0–4 trades/month.
Why this edge could persist
If trend effects exist, they persist partly because they are a form of risk-taking and behavioral
underreaction, not free money. 
Many investors cannot follow systematic rules through drawdowns; trend systems typically require
discipline through whipsaws.
What kind of traders you are “exploiting”
Not a specific group with a structural handicap (like slow prediction-market retail). You are harvesting
aggregate effects arising from:
institutional constraints,
slow-moving capital,
behavioral underreaction.
This is why the edge is modest and not arbitrage-like.

Hostile Risk Review and Profit Reality Check
Top failure modes (hostile reviewer)
1) You think you have alpha, but you actually have a backtest artifact. Trend systems are sensitive to
lookback, execution assumptions, and survivorship if you expand universe beyond ETFs. 
2) Strategy whipsaw: In choppy markets trend flips can cause repeated small losses; volatility targeting can
reduce this but not eliminate it. 
3)  Execution mismatch: Your “near close” execution isn’t the closing print; you get auction slippage or
spread costs that invert expected edge.
4)  Cash-account settlement constraints: Even with T+1, you can create violations if you inadvertently
design frequent buy-sell cycles that rely on unsettled funds. 
5) PDT exposure by accident: If you add intraday exits/entries later , you can accidentally create day trades
and lock the account under PDT restrictions. 
6) Data quality: Splits/dividends/adjustments and missing bars silently corrupt signals.
7) Broker outages / API changes: Your bot fails closed vs fails open. (Failing open is catastrophic.)
8) Monitoring failure: The bot “runs” but stops trading or doubles trades due to a scheduling bug.
9)  Regime  shift: Trend  underperforms  for  years  in  certain  regimes;  your  small  account  can’t  survive
psychologically if you expect “substantial” returns. 
10)  You  chase  leverage: The  temptation  to  use  margin  or  leveraged  ETFs  to  get  “substantial  money”
introduces gap risk and drawdowns that blow up a small account.
Legal/regulatory risk checklist (U.S.)
PDT rule constraints on margin day trading and the $25k equity threshold must be respected. 
Margin minimum equity and broker-specific margin requirements apply; FINRA describes key
baseline requirements in Rule 4210. 
Short sale compliance is governed under Regulation SHO; retail bots should avoid building around
shorting in week one. 
Regulatory landscape can change: FINRA has filed proposed changes that may eliminate PDT
designation mechanics in the future, but that is proposed, not guaranteed or implemented today.
Treat current rules as binding. 
When you should stop running it
Stop the system (or force it into “paper only”) if:
You hit the max drawdown stop (10% from peak) twice within 60 trading days.
You have >3 consecutive execution failures/rejects.
Your realized performance is indistinguishable from noise after costs (e.g., 3 months of flat-to-
negative returns with turnover).

Profit reality check (for $100–$1,000)
This is where “substantial money” collides with arithmetic:
With spot equities/ETFs at 1x notional, even a very good retail strategy might net 0.5%–3% per
month in favorable regimes and negative months in unfavorable regimes. (This is not a promise;
it’s a broad plausibility range for low-frequency systematic strategies.)
On $100, that’s pennies to a few dollars per month.
On $1,000, that’s a few dollars to a few tens of dollars per month, with drawdowns that can easily be
10%+ in bad periods.
To get genuinely “substantial” dollar profits, you need at least one of:
much more capital,
leverage (margin/options), which brings PDT/margin-call/tail risks, 
or a differentiated edge that retail generally does not have (latency/data/structural advantage),
which is typically not achievable with home hardware and public broker APIs. 
Assumptions (explicit)
These are assumptions, not facts:
1) You can obtain clean daily OHLCV for your chosen ETF reliably from your broker or a stable endpoint.
2) Your broker supports fractional shares or your position sizes are large enough to avoid extreme rounding
error .
3) You can execute near the close with acceptable slippage (you may not).
4) Taxes are ignored in expected returns; in reality, frequent rebalances can create short-term gains taxed at
higher rates.
