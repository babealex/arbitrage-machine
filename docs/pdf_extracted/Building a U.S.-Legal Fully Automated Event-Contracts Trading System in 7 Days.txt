Executive Summary
This report designs a single “best V1” automated trading system that is (a) realistically buildable in one week
by a solo builder , (b) runnable 24/7 on an always-on home computer , (c) deployable from the U.S. on
regulated venues, and (d) grounded in a mechanical edge rather than “opinions about the world.”
The key constraint for small capital ($100–$1,000) is fee + spread drag. On Kalshi, taker fees are highest
near 50/50 and lower toward the edges because the fee formula scales with . The published fee
schedule gives taker fees as  and maker fees (where applicable) as 
, rounded up to the next cent.   This makes many “theoretical” prediction-market edges
non-viable  for  micro  accounts  unless  you  (1)  trade  only  when  mispricings  are  several  cents,  or  (2)
preferentially provide liquidity rather than cross the spread.
The single best V1 strategy
Kalshi “monotonicity arbitrage” on Fed Funds threshold ladders (nested events), implemented as two-
leg vertical spreads when prices violate basic set-inclusion logic.
Why this wins for week one:
It targets a structural inequality that must hold by contract logic (subset events can’t be more likely
than supersets), rather than relying on a forecast model.
It is two-leg (not 5–15 legs), which is crucial for reliability under real-world partial fills.
It naturally focuses on tail / illiquid strikes where stale quotes are most likely; a 2026 Federal
Reserve  staff paper highlights that the “outermost (tail) contracts often suffer from low trading
volume,” which can lead to stale pricing, and notes enforcing monotonicity is a practical mitigation
for tail distortions. 
It is buildable with Kalshi’s public market data + authenticated trading API, including WebSocket
order book deltas and order placement with IOC/FOK. 
The rest of the report supports that choice, compares U.S.-accessible venues, ranks mismatch types, and
provides exact rules, architecture, a 7‑day plan, and a hostile risk review.

Platform Comparison
Legal and regulatory baseline for “U.S.-deployable”
For U.S. retail automated trading, the safest baseline is: trade on a  CFTC-designated contract market
(DCM)  with  clearing  via  a  derivatives  clearing  organization (DCO),  within  a  framework  of  federal
derivatives oversight. 
Polymarket  note  (because  you  mentioned  it): the  Commodity  Futures  Trading  Commission took
enforcement action in 2022 against the operator of Polymarket, including a $1.4M penalty and an order to
wind down noncompliant markets and cease and desist from violations.  That history is why “VPN it” is
not an acceptable design requirement for a legally deployable system.

Best V1 Strategy
Strategy name
Kalshi Fed Funds Ladder Monotonicity Arbitrage (Two-leg Vertical Spreads)
Strategy idea in one sentence
Identify  nested-threshold  mispricings and  execute  long-cheaper-superset  /  short-expensive-subset
spreads when the entry credit exceeds all fees and buffers, producing  nonnegative terminal payoff by
construction.
The inefficiency you are exploiting
Kalshi macro markets often exist as many separate binary contracts across strikes; the Federal
Reserve paper describes this “series of binary options that pay $1 if the realized outcome exceeds a
specified strike.” 
In tails and lower-liquidity strikes, quotes can be stale and violate monotonic relationships; the paper
calls out tail staleness and explicitly discusses enforcing monotonicity. 
Many participants are not running “constraint satisfaction” arbitrage bots; they place bids/asks on a
single strike they care about.
Why it persists
Fee + spread friction discourages arbitrage unless mispricing is multiple cents. Kalshi’s fee schedule
makes midpoint trading expensive (fee peaks near 50¢), so small violations may not be worth it for
many traders. 
Operational friction: even if a violation exists, capturing it requires automation + correct
interpretation of the “bids-only / reciprocity” order book representation.

Execution rules that are actually buildable in 7 days
Order  submission  primitives -  Use  POST /portfolio/orders for  each  order  (or  batch  APIs  once
stable). 
- Use time_in_force=fill_or_kill or immediate_or_cancel. 
V1 execution sequence (minimize worst-case blowups) 1.  Buy the lower strike first (the “superset”
event) using FOK: - Buy YES on   at  ,  time_in_force=fill_or_kill, size  . 2. If buy fills,
immediately send sell on the higher strike  at  with IOC. 3. If the sell does not fill: - Try up to
2 more IOC steps down the bid ladder (worse sell price) but only while your recalculated  stays ≥ 0. - If
still unfilled, immediately flatten the long  position (sell YES) using IOC and accept the small loss; this is
your “legging fail safe.”
This is not perfect, but it’s a realistic week-one approach that avoids ever being naked short the higher-
strike contract.
Position sizing rules
Starting capital is small, so size for survivability:
Default size: 1 contract per spread until you have at least 50–100 successful paper executions. 
Scale rule: once stable, size  by available depth and account equity:
i.e., cap at 2% of equity per spread per leg, because platform/settlement risk is not “zero.”
Risk limits
Max open gross notional: 30% of account equity (sum of max downside exposures).
Max daily realized loss: 2% of equity (after which bot disables trading and alerts you).
Max concurrent ladders: 1 meeting initially (keep complexity low).
No-trade window:
from 30 minutes before last trading time through 30 minutes after (Fed-related contracts describe
last trading at 1:55 PM ET and expiration around 2:05 PM ET). 
and any time WebSocket feed is disconnected or sequence numbers are broken.
Expected frequency of trades
Realistically (and conservatively): - Early days: 0–3 trades/day, often clustered in tail strikes. - Many days:
zero (that’s normal for an arbitrage-like strategy).

Risk Analysis, Profit Expectations, and Final Recommendation
Hostile risk review: top reasons this fails
You didn’t actually define a valid subset/superset relationship, because the contract language
differs (meeting definition, “upper bound” detail, strictly greater vs ≥, etc.). Kalshi’s FED contract
explicitly uses the upper bound of the FFR target range and “strictly greater than/less than”
language. 
Legging risk: buy leg fills, sell leg fails; you carry directional exposure. 
Fee underestimation due to rounding behavior; Kalshi publishes fee rounding details and fee
rebates behavior; naive models will be wrong at small sizes. 
Liquidity traps: the bid you intend to hit disappears during your order submission latency.
Stale data / desynced order book after WebSocket reconnect; you trade on bad state.
Slippage dominates: even if monotonicity is violated by 1–2 cents, the spread and fees eliminate
edge (common for small accounts).
Regime shift in microstructure: market maker programs, fee multipliers, or new product design
reduces violations (Kalshi publishes series fee changes and non-standard fee multipliers on its fee
schedule pages). 
Operational outages / API changes: Kalshi ships API changes (e.g., WebSocket channel behavior
adjustments in changelog). 
Settlement timing and disputes: Kalshi notes settlement can take hours after outcome is known
and can take longer when awaiting official data. 
Legal/product eligibility drift: The event-contract space is actively litigated/regulated (especially
sports), and product availability can change quickly; staying in macro reduces this risk, but it’s not
zero. 
Conditions where the strategy loses money
Even though the terminal payoff of the  intended spread is nonnegative, you can lose money in practice
when:
You enter trades where the net credit does not cover fees (common if you chase tiny violations).
You repeatedly suffer legging failures and pay the spread to flatten.
You trade close to expiration when books are jumping and fills are chaotic.
You are wrong about the contract mapping (meeting/threshold parsing bug) and your “spread” is not
actually nested.

More than 2 legging failures in a day requiring flattening at a loss.

Profit reality check
Because you asked for brutal honesty:
Edge per trade (realistic): often 1–5 cents per spread net when it exists, sometimes more in
extreme stale tail quotes; frequently 0 (no trade). This is the natural scale given $0.01 tick sizes and
fee structure. 
Monthly return range (small capital):
If you get a few clean fills: maybe +1% to +10% on $100–$1,000 (that’s $1–$100), but many months
will be lower because opportunities are sporadic.
Bad months can be negative if you overtrade small violations or get repeated legging losses.
Variance/drawdowns: dominated by operational errors and legging, not “market direction” (if you
adhere to the rules).
Scalability limits: liquidity at mispriced tail strikes and the fact that repeated arbitrage quickly
corrects itself. Also, fees climb toward mid-probabilities, so scaling into broad 50/50 markets can
destroy EV. 
Why “$1k → $200k” stories are misleading
Claims  like  that  imply  either:  -  massive  leverage  (not  consistent  with  fully  collateralized  retail  event
contracts), or - sustained very high edge per trade at scale (unlikely in small-tick, fee-bearing markets), or -
survivorship bias (people report wins, not the many failed bots).
In a fully collateralized environment, your growth rate is fundamentally constrained by: available mispricing,
fills,  and  time-to-settlement  capital  lock.  Kalshi  emphasizes  full  collateralization  and  funds  staying  on-
platform while holding positions. 
Final recommendation
If your goal is the “single best” realistic automated system you can build in 7 days from the U.S. with $100–
$1,000:
Build on Kalshi first (demo → production), because the API + WebSockets + demo environment are
built for programmatic trading and are the fastest path to a working autopilot system. 
Trade rates/Fed threshold ladders with the two-leg monotonicity arbitrage strategy described
above, because it is the most “edge-real” and implementation-feasible approach that does not
depend on forecasting models, and is supported by the documented structure and known tail-
staleness issues in these markets.
