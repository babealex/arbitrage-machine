Designing a Retail-Capital, Fully Automated
Options Trading System With Sustainable Positive
Expected Value
Realistic edge sources under retail constraints
A retail system with $5K–$50K, no latency advantage, and standard data cannot compete on the same
axis  as  market  makers  or  “top-of-book”  liquidity  providers,  whose  edge  is  often  dominated  by  spread
capture, queue position, and ultra-low-latency hedging. In listed U.S. options, quotes and last sales are
consolidated and disseminated via Options Price Reporting Authority, which is foundational for how
most participants see the market, but does not grant a meaningful execution edge to a slower , retail-style
automated trader . 
So a plausible path to positive expected value (EV) after costs must be anchored in structural premia
and slow-moving, repeatable conditional effects, not “predict direction, buy calls.” In options, the most
empirically persistent candidates are:
Variance risk premium (VRP): the difference between risk-neutral expected variance (embedded in
option prices) and real-world expected realized variance. This is frequently framed as “implied
variance tends to exceed subsequent realized variance,” meaning sellers of variance/volatility may
earn compensation for warehousing crash/volatility risk (but with negative skew, tail risk). 
Skewness / tail risk premia: equity index option surfaces often show strongly negative skew (puts
rich vs calls), consistent with priced downside jump/crash risk and demand pressure for protection. 
Event-driven volatility term structure effects: scheduled events (earnings; macro releases)
systematically reshape the implied-vol term structure; implied volatility often rises into an event and
drops after uncertainty resolves, though the distribution is very non-Gaussian and instrument-
specific. 
This leads to an important design principle:  your “alpha” is mostly a pricing/structure problem, not a
forecasting problem. You win (if you can) by (a) choosing the correct structure to monetize a premium (or a
mispricing) while (b) constraining the tail and model-error exposure tightly enough to survive inevitable bad
regimes.

and EV

and risk controls. The goal is not a perfect market model; it is a coherent model that (i) respects no-arbitrage
basics, (ii) produces realistic P&L distributions, and (iii) explicitly prices in costs and execution uncertainty.

Risk-neutral  vs  real-world  distributions  (and  why  option  prices  are  not  probabilities). Under  no-
arbitrage pricing, option value is a discounted expectation under a risk-neutral measure, not the real-world
(“physical”) distribution. This distinction is central in Douglas Breeden and Robert Litzenberger: the
(risk-neutral) state price density can be inferred from the curvature of call prices across strikes (intuition: the
second derivative of price w.r .t. strike relates to the risk-neutral density). 
But risk-neutral density is  not a true probability forecast—risk premia distort it. Variance and skewness
premia are documented and modeled in the literature: variance risk premium measurement and replication
via  option  portfolios  (Peter  Carr and  Liuren  Wu),  and  empirical  evidence  that  a  variance  risk
premium has predictive/asset-pricing relevance (e.g., Torben Bollerslev and coauthors). 
Operational takeaway: your EV engine must specify  a real-world distribution   (your best forecast) to
compute expected P&L, while still using IV surfaces for mark-to-market realism.

For event volatility, both the empirical literature and practical trading experience show implied volatility

Execution & microstructure math (spread, fill probability, and impact). The system must treat execution
as stochastic. Two foundations:
Bid–ask spread as a first-order cost: a marketable order crosses the spread; limit orders trade fill-
probability for price improvement. 
Market impact / execution risk models: the Almgren–Chriss framework formalizes trading cost vs
risk and is still the baseline reference class for “execution as optimization.” 
For retail options, impact is often less about moving the underlying and more about crossing wide option
spreads and adverse selection, so your cost model should be empirical and per-underlying/per-DTE.
Portfolio and risk math. You need a sizing rule that explicitly references tail risk. The theoretical Kelly
criterion (maximize expected log wealth) originates with John Larry Kelly Jr .. 
In practice, a retail options system should use  fractional Kelly or a  vol-targeting proxy because (i) your
parameter estimates are noisy, (ii) option tails are hard to model, and (iii) margin/assignment discontinuities
break smooth assumptions.
A buildable regime-safe sizing target is:
compute predicted portfolio daily vol  from scenario P&L,
pick target daily vol  (e.g., 0.4%–0.8% of NAV/day for small accounts),
scale new risk so that ,
enforce a hard max-loss budget per position and per day (see Success Criteria).
Execution realism and constraints that must be engineered in
Market data reality. In U.S.-listed options, consolidated quotation / last-sale dissemination is handled by
OPRA. Your system should log the exact bid/ask it saw at decision time, because options markets are quote-
driven and can be noisy. 
Bid–ask spreads are not a rounding error. Equity option bid–ask spreads are empirically related to option-
level risk/liquidity characteristics; spreads widen for harder-to-hedge options and during stress.

That means “mid fills” are not guaranteed—even in liquid names—and spread costs can flip EV sign for
small  edges.  Your  system  must  treat  spread  and  fill  quality  as  first-class  prediction  targets,  not
afterthoughts.
Limit  vs  market  order  decision. Academic  and  practitioner  summaries  agree  on  the  basic  trade-off:
marketable orders pay the spread for immediacy; limit orders may improve price but risk not filling. 
For retail automation, a robust default is:
use limit orders, pegged to mid or slightly worse than mid,
cap the time you are willing to “chase” (to avoid adverse selection),
cancel if the surface features (IV/skew/spread) drift beyond thresholds while waiting.
If you use a broker that exposes execution tooling, you can optionally route via broker algos designed to
work inside the spread; for example, Interactive Brokers documents an “Adaptive” algo intended to
trade between bid and ask, with urgency controls. 
Assignment/exercise discontinuities are real risk. Short American-style equity options can be assigned;
the mechanics involve clearing through Options Clearing Corporation and assignment obligations that
can create overnight/gap exposure. FINRA explains assignment as the writer’s obligation triggered
when a holder exercises. 
Automation requirements that follow from this:
avoid holding short ITM options near expiration unless explicitly modeled and margin-safe,
include dividend/exercise heuristics (or simply ban short calls over ex-div windows),
close structures before expiration unless the system has explicit exercise-by-exception handling.
Retail  constraints:  day-trading  rules  and  capital  efficiency. If  the  system  intends  to  trade  intraday
frequently in a margin account under $25K, the pattern day trader regime matters. FINRA states a $25,000
minimum equity requirement for pattern day traders. 
A realistic fully automated retail options system should therefore bias toward  swing horizons (days to
weeks), not minute-level churn, unless the user’s account structure and broker rules explicitly support it.

Layer F — Learning loop
You need three labels per trade, not one:
Thesis label: did the hypothesized phenomenon occur? 
example for earnings vol: did IV crush happen relative to baseline? 
example for VRP capture: was realized variance lower than implied over the holding horizon? 
Structure label: did the chosen structure convert the thesis into P&L efficiently? (theta/vega/gamma
profile correctness) 
Execution label: did fills match the assumed cost model? (spread paid, missed fills, adverse
selection)
Then update:
execution cost model (per underlying/DTE/time-of-day)

regime classifier thresholds (when does the strategy stop trading?)
A critical empirical caution: option quote data can be noisy, and microstructure biases can distort inference
if you naïvely treat mid quotes as executable prices.

Earnings volatility trades
Core  idea: trade  the  difference  between  (a)  event-implied  move  priced  into  the  front  expiry  and  (b)
expected realized move plus post-event IV dynamics.
Empirical  anchors:  implied  volatility  behavior  and  changes  around  earnings  are  documented;  implied
volatility often drops after earnings on average, consistent with uncertainty resolution. 
Most automation-feasible structures:
Calendars/diagonals: short the near-term event vol, long the back-month to reduce tail blow-up
from a single jump; profits from IV crush concentrated in front expiry and term-structure
normalization.
Defined-risk iron condors (only in very liquid names): monetize inflated event IV but cap disaster via
wings; still exposed to gap risk beyond wings.
Competition: high in megacaps; moderate in mid-liquidity names but spreads get worse (costs rise). The
optimizer must explicitly reject trades where spread costs consume edge.
Scalability: moderate; small accounts can play 1-lot calendars/spreads; scaling is mostly linear until liquidity
constraints appear .
Post-earnings drift using options
Post-earnings-announcement drift (PEAD) is a documented phenomenon in the equity literature; recent
reviews summarize the evidence and its persistence/attenuation. 
But  options  overlay  is  tricky: immediately  after  earnings,  IV  often  collapses;  directional  options  can
become cheaper , but spreads can still be wide and the drift signal is weak/noisy.
Automation-feasible implementation (if you do it at all):
Use the LLM to classify earnings text into “high surprise likelihood” + sentiment direction, but then
express via vertical spreads (defined risk) rather than outright long calls/puts.
Require that directional EV remains positive after conservative spread assumptions; reject if IV/skew
pricing implies too much premium.
Competition: extremely high; drift is widely known; edge is likely small and regime-dependent.
Scalability: limited; works best on liquid names with tight spreads.
Index volatility and VRP harvesting (SPY/QQQ class)
The most intellectually coherent “retail quant” route is  systematic volatility risk premium capture with
tight risk controls. The variance risk premium is extensively studied and can be proxied by the difference
between option-implied variance (variance swap replication) and realized variance.

Structures that fit retail constraints:
Defined-risk put spreads / iron condors at 30–60 DTE, sized small, rebalanced weekly; avoid short-
dated high gamma.
Crash-aware overlays: allocate a small, persistent tail hedge budget (e.g., small far-OTM puts) to
mitigate regime kill; this reduces EV but stabilizes survival odds.
Competition: high,  but  VRP  is  a  risk  premium—not  a  “free  lunch  mispricing.”  The  question  becomes
whether your system can capture it without being wiped out.
Scalability: strong up to retail size; liquidity is deep in major index ETFs, but you still pay spreads.
Macro event trades (CPI, FOMC)
Macro event schedules are known in advance (e.g., CPI release dates by BLS; FOMC meeting calendar by the
Fed). 
Edge hypothesis: markets may systematically over/underprice event risk in the front expiry, creating term-
structure kinks that a calendar/diagonal can monetize—if your event jump model is calibrated and your
execution costs are controlled.
Best structures:
Short-front / long-back calendars around scheduled releases (avoid ultra-short 0DTE; you have no
latency).
Avoid pure short straddles unless you have strict disaster caps and high confidence in cost model.
Competition: very high (macro options flows are heavily professionalized).
Automation  feasibility: medium;  calendars  are  automation-friendly;  risk  management  must  be
conservative.
Skew exploitation (put spreads vs call spreads)
Downside skew and skew risk premia are documented in equity index options; the literature ties skew to
priced crash/jump risk and demand pressure. 
Edge  hypothesis: relative-value  opportunities  in  skew  steepness  (e.g.,  put  spread  pricing  vs  expected
downside tail under your physical model) may appear in certain regimes.
Automation-feasible tactic:
Fit skew features from the IV surface.
Trade vertical put spreads instead of naked puts to cap gap risk.
Require edge robustness under stress scenarios (higher jump probability).
Competition: high; skew is “expensive” for a reason; you are being paid (if at all) to take tail risk.

Calendar spreads and term-structure mispricing as the primary niche
If  you  must  pick  one  niche  that  is  both  (a)  automatable  and  (b)  not  purely  latency-driven,  it  is  term-
structure relative value (calendars/diagonals) around events and regimes.
Why: it naturally emphasizes structure selection, explicitly depends on vol surface + term structure, and
can be engineered to have better worst-case behavior than naked short-vol. It also matches the “surface +
scenario engine + optimizer” architecture you want.
What fails and why these approaches reliably blow up
Direction-only prediction models. The failure mode is not that predicting direction is impossible; it is that
even a decent directional signal can have negative options EV if you ignore IV and convexity. Option prices
embed implied volatility and higher-moment pricing; paying too much for convexity is the fastest way to
lose despite being “right” about direction. 
LLM-only trading systems. LLM outputs are not calibrated probability distributions over returns, and they
are especially weak on market microstructure and cost modeling. In this architecture, LLMs are constrained
to produce structured features (event classification, uncertainty), never trades.
Ignoring implied volatility surface. If you don’t model skew and term structure, you cannot even define
what  “cheap”  or  “rich”  means  in  options.  Practical  smile  modeling  (SABR/SVI  families)  exists  precisely
because constant-vol assumptions fail in real markets. 
Ignoring spreads/slippage. Many option EV edges are on the order of a few basis points of notional per
day; spreads can be larger than the modeled edge, especially for far OTM or low-liquidity series. Spread/
liquidity variation in options is empirically meaningful, and microstructure noise can distort inference. 
Overfitting via backtests and “strategy factories.” This is a dominant failure mode in systematic trading
research: testing many variants on the same data creates selection bias and data snooping. The “Probability
of Backtest Overfitting” framework (David H. Bailey and coauthors) formalizes why picking the best
backtest among many tends to disappoint out of sample; Halbert White’s “Reality Check” addresses data
snooping in model selection. 
A  production  system  must  therefore  treat  evaluation  as  a  controlled  experiment  (walk-forward,  strict
separation, limited hyperparameter freedom), not a backtest leaderboard.

Options chain snapshots: at least daily end-of-day chain; plus higher frequency (e.g., hourly or
every few minutes) on trade decision windows to log what you could actually execute.
For each option series in the snapshot: bid, ask, last, size (if available), open interest, volume. OPRA

Event calendar: earnings dates (from filings/vendor); macro schedule (Fed + BLS calendars). 
Filings ingestion: EDGAR API access for automated event tagging and document retrieval. 
Execution logs: every order attempt, quote at submission time, fill/no-fill, time-to-fill, commissions/

Without execution logs, you cannot learn realistic slippage models; without chain snapshots, you cannot
reconstruct what the optimizer “saw.”
Ideal dataset
The ideal dataset reduces model risk and lets you model fill probability better:
Higher-frequency option quote/trade data around execution (millisecond not required; but sub-
minute snapshots help). 
Per-leg execution data and order book proxies (if your broker provides). 
Dividend data and corporate actions, to model early exercise risk for American options. 
Cross-asset vol indicators (e.g., volatility indices, rates) to improve regime classification; Cboe
volatility index methodology documents the concept of model-free implied variance from option
prices. 
What can be approximated vs required
Required: bid/ask at decision time; otherwise your cost model is fantasy. 
Approximatable: risk-free rate (short rate proxy), detailed order book depth (can be approximated
by spread + size + historical fill stats), and full-tick OPRA (expensive) if you’re not doing high-
frequency.

Month one
Implement one constrained strategy family with conservative rules:
Pick a single universe (e.g., 2–4 very liquid underlyings only).
Start with calendar spreads around scheduled events (earnings or macro) where the edge
hypothesis is “front vol mean-reverts/crushes.” 
Implement strict no-trade filters (spread thresholds, OI thresholds, time-to-expiry bands).
Validate execution model: measure realized spread paid vs assumed.
Deliverable: a monthly report generated automatically with realized EV decomposition: - theoretical edge
(model EV)
- spread/fees slippage
- residual (model error)
Month two to three
Expand modeling where it matters:
Add regime classification features (IV term structure + RV) and make trading conditional on regimes.
Upgrade scenario engine with calibrated event jumps and IV crush distributions per event type. 
Add alternative structures (iron condor , vertical spreads) but keep template count small to avoid
combinatorial overfitting. 
Add explicit tail stress tests (jump size ×2, IV crush ×0.5, spread ×1.5).
Deliverable: optimizer chooses among 3–6 structure templates with a stable EV/CVaR objective.
