A Quant Team Playbook for U.S.-Legal Automated
Event-Contract Trading
Executive Summary
A small retail account ($100–$1,000) running on an always-on home machine can build a fully automated
event-contract system in days, but the set of durable, implementable edges is narrow. The most robust
edges  are  not  “forecasting  the  world  better”  but  exploiting  structural  constraints  and  cross-market
mapping errors that persist because of  fees, fragmentation, thin books, and retail participation—
especially  in  laddered  economic  contracts  where  many  discrete  markets  must  collectively  encode  a
coherent distribution. 
From a U.S.-legal standpoint, the core venue is Kalshi, which the Commodity Futures Trading Commission
 designated as a contract market (DCM) in 2020, making it the cleanest “retail-accessible + regulated +
API-friendly” prediction-market-like venue from the United States. 
This playbook focuses on:
True probability modeling for macro events (Fed decisions, CPI, unemployment) using professional
market-implied frameworks (Fed funds futures, OIS, options-implied distributions, surveys).
Microstructure realities for Kalshi (primary), plus Polymarket and PredictIt as comparative case
studies (with U.S. legal constraints clearly noted).
Execution in thin books (the #1 failure point for small automated traders).
A complete arbitrage/mismatch landscape with implementability notes, robust signal filters, and
sizing/risk control suitable for $100–$10,000.
Bias-resistant validation methods when historical data is sparse.
A professional-grade system blueprint and a roadmap from $1,000 to $10,000+ that does not rely on
fantasy returns.
Facts vs assumptions
Facts (documented by primary sources used here): - CME FedWatch derives unconditional probabilities
of FOMC outcomes from 30‑Day Fed Funds futures, assumes 25 bp increments, and provides explicit step-
by-step  formulas  for  building  a  binary  probability  tree.   -  OIS  floating  legs  are  based  on  daily
compounded overnight rates, and (for “vanilla” short-maturity U.S. OIS) cashflows are typically netted and
exchanged  at  maturity.   -  Kalshi’s  fee  schedule  is  price-dependent:  taker  fees  are
ceil(0.07 × C × P × (1–P)) (maker fees ceil(0.0175 × C × P × (1–P)) where applicable).

True Probability Modeling for Macro Events
Professional “true probabilities” are almost always  market-implied or  model-implied probabilities under
explicit measures and assumptions—not metaphysical truth. The correct posture is:
Derive a risk-neutral (or risk-adjusted) distribution from macro-linked instruments.
Apply a contract mapping layer into the event-contract payoff definitions.
Trade only when the edge exceeds fees + execution risk + model error by a wide margin.

How pros extract probabilities: In practice, desks fit an OIS curve (discount factors / forward rates),
interpret it as a  risk-neutral expectation of short-term rates plus term premia, and derive meeting-to-
meeting expectations by comparing forward segments around policy dates. The Bank of England notes
that (in deep/liquid markets) OIS rates should reflect investors’ expectations of future short-term rates,
while acknowledging term premia especially at longer horizons. 
Options-implied distributions
Options encode a full risk-neutral distribution (not just a mean) of an underlying variable. The canonical
technique is the Breeden–Litzenberger relationship: under idealized assumptions, the risk-neutral density
can be extracted from the second derivative of call prices with respect to strike (properly discounted).
Central bank and international-institution research documents this approach and emphasizes estimation
technique choices.

Economist surveys and forecast distributions
Surveys  are  used  operationally  as:  -  a  reference  “consensus  mean/median,”  -  a  dispersion  proxy
(uncertainty), - and an anchor to separate expectations from term/risk premia in market-implied rates.
Rate-expectations  research  explicitly  discusses  survey-augmented  approaches  (for  example,  contrasting
OIS-augmented vs survey-augmented term-structure identification methods). 
A practical survey-to-distribution mapping for an event  (CPI print, payrolls, unemployment rate) is often: -
assume   where   is  consensus  and   is  calibrated  from  survey  dispersion  +  historical
forecast errors, - then compute interval probabilities by integrating the distribution across contract bins.

Fees are nonlinear and state-dependent: taker fees peak near 50/50 and shrink toward the
extremes per the published parabolic formula; this changes who trades, where liquidity
concentrates, and what mispricings are economically tradable.

Market Microstructure Where Edge Hides
Kalshi microstructure highlights
Order book representation: Kalshi’s order book returns bids only and relies on the reciprocal relationship
between YES and NO. A “NO bid at price ” corresponds to a “YES ask at .” This is not a UI quirk—it
fundamentally affects how you compute executable prices and spreads. 
Order  types  and  constraints  (API-level): -  time_in_force supports  fill_or_kill, 
immediate_or_cancel, good_till_canceled.  - buy_max_cost forces fill-or-kill behavior when
provided (critical for multi-leg risk control).  - post_only exists (maker-style behavior). 
Fee structure: Kalshi fees follow the published parabolic formula and rounding rules; this has second-order
effects like “minimum 1-cent fee cliffs” for very small sizes, and it changes the break-even spread required
for makers vs takers. 
API/contract  representation  changes  are  real  operational  risk: Kalshi’s  March  2026  “fixed-point
migration” introduced subpenny pricing fields and fractional contract sizes, requiring careful handling of
decimals and fee rounding math. If you ignore this, your bot misprices edges and mis-sizes orders. 
Polymarket microstructure (comparative, with U.S. legality caveat)
Polymarket describes a CLOB where orders are signed messages, matched offchain, and settle onchain;
users can stream market data via WebSockets (market channel streams orderbook changes, price updates,
trades). 
However ,  U.S.-retail legality is constrained: the CFTC’s 2022 order required the operator to pay a civil
monetary  penalty  and  wind  down  noncompliant  markets  and  cease  violations,  widely  interpreted  as
requiring blocking U.S. users for those markets.

Why inefficiencies persist and when they worsen
Inefficiencies persist in event markets for structural reasons:
Fragmentation across bins/strikes: the “true distribution” is split across many small markets, so
enforcing global consistency requires specialized bots.
Thin depth and infrequent updates: small markets can go stale.
Retail-driven flow: more sentiment and less constraint-based arbitrage.
Nonlinear fees: small mispricings are not worth arbitraging after fees. 
Conditions that typically increase inefficiency (high-confidence, but still something you should measure): -
long-dated markets, - tail strikes/bins, - low-attention windows (overnight, weekends), - immediately after
new contract listings or rule/schema changes (e.g., pricing/quantity precision changes). 
Execution and Arbitrage Playbook
Execution is the dominant failure mode for small automated systems in thin books: you can be directionally
correct and still lose money by paying spreads, fees, and legging losses.
Optimal execution in thin books
IOC vs FOK vs passive limits (Kalshi API specifics)
FOK (fill_or_kill): best for “must be complete” legs, especially when your model edge is small.
Kalshi supports FOK via time_in_force=fill_or_kill. 
IOC (immediate_or_cancel): useful when partial fills are acceptable or when you are probing
available liquidity quickly; explicitly supported by Kalshi. 
Passive maker (post_only, typically with GTC): lowers fees (maker fee tier where applicable) but
increases adverse-selection risk—especially for time-sensitive macro events. Kalshi supports 
post_only. 
Cost caps: buy_max_cost enforces FOK-like behavior with a defined max spend—useful for “do
not exceed edge budget” execution.

Legging risk in multi-leg trades
Event-market arbitrage frequently requires multiple legs (partition, ladder spreads, cross-market hedges).
Without atomic multi-leg execution, you face:
partial fills,
stale price movement between legs,

Best practice for small accounts: prefer two-leg structures where one leg can be safely held temporarily
or cheaply unwound, and require large pre-fee edge margins before attempting >2 legs.

Cross-Market Mismatch Engineering
Cross-market mismatch is often the single best “real edge” category because it anchors you to an external
reference distribution that is not purely Kalshi’s internal consensus.
Mapping Kalshi contracts to external macro instruments
Fed decisions: from FedWatch distribution to Kalshi contracts
The  cleanest  mapping  target  is  the  distribution  over  possible  target-rate  outcomes  implied  by  CME
FedWatch (or your own replication of its method).
Workflow:
Build CME-style distribution for the relevant meeting date (binary tree across meetings if needed).
CME’s methodology explicitly defines how to compute month-average EFFR from futures, solve start/
end rates via day weights, and convert expected changes into discrete probabilities in 25 bp
increments. 
Translate EFFR outcomes into the event-contract settlement definition:
If the contract is about “will the target range be X–Y,” map to a midpoint or upper-bound convention
consistent with the contract terms (this is where many mapping errors occur).
Compute a probability for each Kalshi outcome/bin.
CPI and unemployment: practical reality
Retail-accessible “direct” instruments for CPI-print distributions are limited. In practice, sophisticated desks
use combinations of: - surveys and nowcasts for the mean, - historical error distributions for dispersion, -
options-implied distributions on related assets for event risk adjustment, - and (when available) inflation-
linked derivatives to refine the tails.
For a small retail system, CPI/jobs are usually treated as: -  survey + historical surprise distribution,
optionally  adjusted  by  market-implied  risk  measures  (equity  index  implied  volatility  around  release
windows).

Validating mismatches statistically
You want to avoid confusing  noise with  opportunity. Practical validation methods that work even with
limited history:
Out-of-sample “live paper” logging: Record every time  exceeds threshold and whether (a) you
would have been filled at your assumed execution, and (b) the trade’s realized P&L after settlement.
Bootstrap uncertainty for : When  comes from survey distributions, bootstrap historical forecast
errors to generate confidence bands; only trade when mismatch exceeds the upper confidence
bound of model error .
Stability checks across sources: If FedWatch-implied and OIS-implied expectations disagree
materially, lower position size—consensus disagreement is a red flag for hidden premia or mapping
error . 
Examples of real-world mismatch mechanisms
Even without publishing a current trade recommendation, it’s useful to name archetypes:
Bin inconsistency near “adjacent” outcomes: CME method often concentrates probability on two
adjacent 25 bp outcomes in the nearest node; if a prediction market spreads probability across far
tails inconsistently, intermediate bins can be under/overpriced. 
Post-news “stale tails”: When attention shifts to the main outcome, tail bins can remain stale
(especially if fees discourage arbitrage), creating monotonicity or partition violations.

Core behavioral patterns in prediction markets
Overreaction to salient news: rapid shifts to extremes that overshoot (especially when liquidity is
thin).
Anchoring: prices cluster around round numbers or pre-event reference levels, even as external
implied distributions shift.
Herding: follow-the-tape behavior in a small venue where a few orders move the “last price.”
Sentiment lag: prices adjust slower in less attended strike bins even when the central outcome
updates quickly.
Kalshi itself publishes educational content emphasizing how probabilities can move toward extremes as
events unfold and traders update conditional probabilities—mechanically correct, but also a behavioral
driver when traders extrapolate too aggressively. 
Detecting behavioral mispricing algorithmically
A practical framework is to treat behavioral signals as filters, not primary alpha, unless you have evidence.
Implementable detectors:
Dislocation vs external anchor
If the Kalshi price moves > X cents in Y minutes but your external anchor  barely moves (FedWatch/
OIS stable), classify as potential overreaction and reduce aggression on taker orders. 
Order book imbalance

Risk Management, Position Sizing, and Bias-Resistant Validation
Kelly and fractional Kelly for binary contracts
For a YES contract: - You pay  - You receive  if it resolves YES, else 
The expected edge per contract is  (above). The Kelly-optimal fraction depends on the payoff odds
and your probability estimate error . In small, model-uncertain systems, fractional Kelly is standard: use 10–
25% of theoretical Kelly at most.
Practical sizing rule for $100–$10,000:
Base unit risk = 1% of equity per independent bet (or per leg if multi-leg).
For higher uncertainty (cross-market mapping not fully validated), cut to 0.25–0.5%.
Cap total open exposure at 20–30% of equity for an early-stage bot, because platform + operational
+ settlement risks dominate “market risk” at small scale.
Drawdown control and risk of ruin
For small systems, “risk of ruin” is more about operational blowups than pure betting odds.
Non-negotiable controls:
Daily loss limit (realized): 1–2% of equity.
Weekly loss limit: 3–5% of equity.
Max open positions: keep it small until fills/fees are empirically stable.
Kill switch on abnormal conditions:
repeated order rejects,
data feed gaps,
schema mismatches (precision changes).
Kalshi’s fixed-point migration illustrates why schema changes are not theoretical; they happen and can
silently break bots. 
Backtesting without bias in event markets
Event markets often lack long standardized histories. You must treat backtesting itself as an engineering
product.
A practical, bias-resistant validation ladder:
Forward paper trading with full depth capture
Subscribe to real-time order book updates and store snapshots/deltas.
Store your computed signals and the exact orders you would send.

Top failure modes and how to prevent them
Wrong mapping (definition mismatch)
Prevent with contract-term parsing tests and a human-reviewed mapping file; do not assume names
imply identical settlement.
Execution mismatch (paper P&L not achievable live)
Prevent with execution-realistic replay and conservative slippage buffers.
Fee blindness
Prevent  by  calculating  EV  using  the  actual  fee  formula  and  rounding  cliffs;  never  trade  “1-cent
edges.” 
Legging blowups
Prevent by preferring 1–2 leg trades, using FOK on critical legs, and having immediate flatten logic;
Kalshi’s order API supports FOK/IOC and max-cost caps. 
Schema drift (precision changes)
Prevent with schema-version checks and strict numeric typing (decimal fixed-point), especially post
March 2026 changes. 
Stale book / reconnect gaps
Prevent by halting trading after reconnect until a full snapshot is rebuilt.
Overfitting (especially for behavioral signals)
Prevent by using behavioral signals only as filters until proven.
Venue/legal drift
Prevent by sticking to clearly U.S.-legal venues; Polymarket’s history shows enforcement can reshape
access. 
Operational outages
Prevent with heartbeats, auto-restart, and “fail closed” posture.
Capital lock and liquidity traps
Prevent  by  sizing  small,  avoiding  thin  tail  bins  unless  edge  is  large,  and  diversifying  across
independent strategies only after V1 works.

Phase goals (illustrative, not guaranteed):
$1k → $2k: Prove operational reliability; trade only high-confidence signals (monotonicity/partition

Expected setbacks: temporary strategy death when a venue improves liquidity/market making, schema
changes, and periods where external implied distributions disagree or carry large premia—your system
must reduce risk automatically in those periods. 
Optional extension into equities and macro-linked instruments
The “pro” version of cross-market mismatch uses liquid hedges: - rate futures / swaps proxies to hedge Fed
decision exposure, - equity index futures/options as event-risk gauges for CPI/jobs.

full delta-hedged arbitrage, unless you have sufficient capital and permissions.
